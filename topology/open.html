Metric topologies

Let $d: X \times X \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ be a metric, i.e.
  • $ d(x,y)\geq 0 $ for all $x \neq y$ non-negativity
  • $ d(x,y)=0\Leftrightarrow x=y $
  • $d(x,y)=d(y,x)$ symmetry
  • $d(x,z)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,z)$ triangle inequality
Metric topologies have the open balls $B_r(x) = \{ y \in X \mid d(x,y) < r \}$ as basis, i.e. every open set $O$ can be written as an (probably infinite) union of balls. The triangle inequality implies that this is equivalent to: $$\forall x\in O \,\exists \varepsilon>0: B_{\varepsilon}(x) \subset O$$.

Standard-Metric

The standard-metric is induced by the $\ell_{2}$-norm, i.e. $$d(x,y) = ||x-y||_2 = \sqrt{(x_1-y_1)^2+(x_2-y_2)^2}$$

Manhattan distance

THe Manhatten metric is induced by the $\ell_{1}$-norm, i.e. $$d(x,y) = ||x-y||_1 = |x_1-y_1|+|x_2-y_2|$$ The name is motivated by the grid layout of most streets on the island of Manhattan. The Manhatten metric induces the same topology as the standard metric.

SCNF-metric

If you want to travel by train in France, it is very likely that the fastes connection goes through Paris. In $\mathbb{R}^2$ this can be modelled as:
  • $d(x,y)=||x-y||_2$ if $x$ and $y$ have the same phase and
  • $d(x,y)=||x||_2 + ||y||_2$
    • in all other cases (i.e. going through $0$ = Paris)
The induced topological space corresponds to an uncountable a set of disjoint rays joined at a point.

Box topology

A basis of the box topology in $\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}$ can be formed by sets $B_1 \times B_2$ where $B_1$, $B_2$ are basis elements of the standard topology in $\mathbb{R}$. In finite $\mathbb{R}$ vectorspaces, this construction gives the same topology as the one that is induced by the $\ell$-norms
